A cappuccino is espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam in equal parts. The entire definition fits in a sentence. Everything else — the cup, the temperature, the bean, the fat content of the milk — is elaboration on that premise.

This is a specific drink, not approximately a specific drink.

## The three components

**Espresso.** A double shot, roughly 30ml, pulled through fine-ground coffee at around nine bars of pressure in 25–30 seconds. The espresso is the structure of the drink. Its bitterness, its body, its crema are what the milk is tempering — not hiding. If the espresso disappears entirely into the milk, the ratio is wrong somewhere.

**Steamed milk.** Not heated. Not frothed. *Steamed* — which is a specific process where pressure introduces air into cold milk as tiny, suspended bubbles. Done correctly, the result sits somewhere between foam and plain milk: silkier than milk, more integrated than foam, with a sweetness from the lactose that peaks around 60°C. That sweetness is why a cappuccino made properly doesn't need sugar. That sweetness is also what disappears when a barista overshoots to 70° because a customer once complained their drink wasn't hot enough. Past a certain temperature, the milk tastes cooked. You'll know it when you taste it. It's everywhere.

**Foam.** This is the part most café cappuccinos get wrong, and most cappuccino photography gets wrong in the opposite direction. The foam in a correctly made cappuccino is not a decorative layer. It is not the white snowdrift that sits above the coffee and slides off when you tip the cup. It is the texture of the steamed milk itself — microfoam woven through the liquid so completely that the two aren't really separable. When you pour it right, the foam and the milk pour as one thing. By the time you finish the drink, you've consumed the foam too. You shouldn't need a spoon.

What a well-made cappuccino looks like in a white cup: a smooth, slightly matte surface with a brown window in the center where the espresso shows through. Not necessarily latte art. Just integrated and intentional. The cup should be warm to the touch — not scalding, just warm — because a cappuccino in a cold cup drops temperature before the first sip. This is a small thing that makes a noticeable difference.

## The 1:1:1 ratio

Equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and foam. In a cup of 150–180ml.

Not a guideline. Not a tradition that's flexible depending on who you ask. The ratio. The cup size determines everything downstream: start with 30ml of espresso and you have 60ml of milk and 60ml of foam to work with. The whole drink is small. This is intentional. It's designed to be finished in a few minutes while it's still warm enough to taste right.

When a café makes a "cappuccino" in a 250ml cup, they are making a latte. This is not a snobbish position — it's just a description. The ratios produce a specific balance of bitterness, body, and sweetness. More milk changes that balance. It doesn't mean more; it means different, and in this case, flatter.

## The time question

The Italian tradition holds that cappuccinos belong to the morning — before 11am, ideally alongside a cornetto, and under no circumstances after a meal. The official reason involves milk and digestion. The unofficial reason is that this is how it's done and has always been done and therefore how it should continue to be done.

I drink cappuccinos in the afternoon. Often at 3pm. Sometimes later. This has yet to cause any trouble I'm aware of. My stomach has not lodged a complaint.

I bring this up because the 11am convention gets cited with a seriousness it doesn't quite deserve, and occasionally at people who just wanted a coffee. It's a cultural habit from a country whose coffee culture I respect enormously, not a physiological law. Order what you want. Your enjoyment of a cappuccino at 2pm does not diminish anyone else's enjoyment of their 8am one.

## What it tastes like

Made correctly: like espresso, but rounder. The bitterness softens. A sweetness arrives that's from the milk, not sugar. The texture is what distinguishes it from every other espresso drink — each sip has weight to it, a creaminess that isn't thick but that you register.

It should be finished while warm. A cappuccino that cools is a different, worse drink. The milk separates. The sweetness flattens. Whatever was on the surface consolidates into something best not examined. Two minutes is ideal. Five is fine. Much longer than that and you've let it become something it wasn't trying to be.

## Why the ratio isn't arbitrary

The 1:1:1 proportion wasn't designed by a standards committee. It was arrived at by making many cappuccinos and discovering that this particular balance tastes right. The equal thirds are the conclusion, not the starting point.

When a café hands you a 300ml cappuccino and describes it as traditional, the problem isn't rule-breaking. It's that the drink doesn't taste the way the drink tastes. The rule exists because the result it produces is good. That's the whole argument.

These three drinks account for most of what gets ordered at espresso bars, and they get confused constantly — by customers, by cafés, and by menus that use the names interchangeably. The confusion is understandable. All three start with espresso. All three use steamed milk. The differences are in proportions, milk texture, and what those choices do to the way the drink tastes.

This is a practical comparison. Not a definitional argument.

## The cappuccino

A cappuccino is traditionally one part espresso, one part steamed milk, one part dense microfoam — in a 150–180ml cup. The foam isn't decoration. It's a third of the drink. It changes the texture, slows the drink's cooling, and separates the espresso from the palate in a way that makes the bitterness more present but less harsh.

The foam should be dry enough to hold its shape but wet enough to integrate with the milk beneath it. The line between "correctly textured cappuccino foam" and "foam that looks like shaving cream" is real and worth caring about. The former is silky and merges with the espresso at the bottom. The latter sits on top of the drink doing nothing.

<PullQuote text="The foam isn't decoration. It's a third of the drink — and it changes everything about how the espresso registers on the palate." />

What a cappuccino tastes like: espresso-forward. The milk rounds the edges, the foam adds body and slight sweetness, but the espresso is what you're drinking. If your cappuccino tastes mostly like warm milk, something has gone wrong with either the ratio or the espresso extraction.

Volume is the key diagnostic. If you're handed something in a 10oz cup and told it's a cappuccino, it isn't. It might be good. It might be what you wanted. But the proportions that define a cappuccino no longer exist at that volume unless someone pulled three shots, which is rarely what's happening.

## The latte

A latte — caffè latte, "milk coffee" — is espresso with a much larger proportion of steamed milk. The standard range is 240–360ml, with a milk-to-espresso ratio of roughly 3:1 or 4:1. The foam layer is thin, maybe 5mm, and the milk texture is fluid rather than dense — it should be silky and pourable, integrated completely with the espresso rather than sitting above it.

The flavour profile is milk-forward. The espresso is present — you can taste it, especially in the finish — but the milk is the predominant sensation. This is not a criticism. If you want a drink that's smooth, warm, and gentle on the espresso, a latte is the honest answer. The cappuccino is not the "better" drink. It's a different drink.

The latte is also the most forgiving drink to make at home, because the large milk volume hides steaming imperfections. Slightly over-foamed milk that would ruin a cappuccino blends into a latte without much notice. This is probably why home espresso machines get reviewed on latte quality before cappuccino quality — the margin for error is wider.

## The flat white

The flat white is the most contested definition in coffee, largely because its origins are disputed between Australia and New Zealand and every café seems to have its own idea of what it is. For practical purposes: a flat white is a double espresso — often ristretto — in a 150–180ml cup with steamed milk that's been textured to a very fine, velvety microfoam with almost no surface foam.

The distinction from the cappuccino is in the milk texture. A cappuccino has a layer of dense foam as a deliberate component of the drink. A flat white has milk steamed so thoroughly that the foam has been integrated entirely — there's no distinct foam layer, just a surface sheen. The milk is smooth and velvety, not fluffy.

The distinction from the latte is in the volume and the espresso intensity. A flat white is smaller and uses a higher coffee-to-milk ratio, which means the espresso character comes through more clearly. Some cafés use a ristretto double, which makes the espresso even more concentrated and produces a sweeter, less bitter shot that carries through the milk cleanly.

What a flat white tastes like: balanced. The espresso is clear but not dominant. The milk is present but not sweet. When it's made well, it's the most nuanced of the three drinks — the espresso and milk are neither competing nor drowning each other. When it's made badly, it's a small latte with a more expensive name.

The flat white requires the tightest steaming technique of the three. You have a small volume of milk, almost no foam, and a narrow temperature window. A single degree too hot and the milk's sweetness collapses. Ten seconds too long on the air introduction and you've got a cappuccino.

I've had flat whites at three different cafés in the same neighbourhood on the same morning — call it research, call it a bad day — and none of them were the same drink. One was a small latte. One was a ristretto cappuccino without foam. One was genuinely a flat white. Same name on the menu, three different interpretations.

## What the milk texture actually does

The three drinks represent three different milk textures, and those textures change more than just mouthfeel. They change what you taste.

Dense foam (cappuccino) creates a layer between your palate and the espresso on the first sip. This initially softens the impact, but as the foam melts into the espresso through the cup, you get an increasing espresso presence as you drink. A cappuccino tends to taste "more" as it progresses.

Fluid microfoam (latte) integrates with the espresso immediately. The sweetness of the milk registers from the first sip. There's no separation, no progression — you get the whole drink at once.

Velvety microfoam (flat white) is the most integrated texture — neither creating a barrier like dense foam nor being so fluid that it simply dilutes. The milk and espresso meet each other as near-equals throughout the cup.

This is also why the cup size matters so much for the cappuccino specifically. Increase the volume by adding more milk, and you've changed the ratio. Change the ratio, and you've changed the balance. Change the balance, and you're no longer drinking the same drink.

## Which one to order

This doesn't require much analysis. If you want espresso to be the point of the drink and you want a small, intense cup: cappuccino. If you want something smooth and milk-forward that you can drink slowly over twenty minutes without the espresso cooling into bitterness: latte. If you want espresso character in a small cup without the foam layer: flat white.

The only wrong answer is ordering one drink and expecting another.

## Making them at home

The cappuccino is the hardest of the three to reproduce at home — not because the technique is complex, but because it requires a steam wand with enough pressure to create genuinely dense microfoam rather than airy bubbles. Most home machines above $400 can do this. Most machines below $200 cannot.

The latte is the most forgiving at home because the milk volume masks mistakes. Slightly over-foamed, slightly under-integrated — it doesn't matter much in a 300ml cup.

The flat white is technically demanding in the opposite direction from the cappuccino: you need fine control to steam a small volume to the right texture without over-heating. The margin for error is narrower than either of the other two.

The common denominator is whole milk, cold into the pitcher. If you're learning milk technique, start with the latte, move to the flat white, then the cappuccino. In that order.

Getting the milk right is the harder half of a cappuccino. The espresso, once dialled in, is repeatable. The milk requires feel — a physical sense of what the pitcher should sound and feel like at each stage of the process.

This is not difficult to learn. It takes about two weeks of daily practice.

## What you need

A steam wand with enough pressure to actually texture milk (most home machines above $400 qualify), a 350ml stainless steel pitcher, and whole milk. Learn on whole milk. Its fat and protein content is the most forgiving. Switch to alternatives only after the technique is solid.

## The process

**Purge the wand first.** Before you steam, open the steam valve for one second with a cloth wrapped around the tip. This expels condensed water that accumulated since the last use. Water in your milk ruins the texture.

**Fill the pitcher.** Cold milk, to just below the bottom of the spout. Around 150ml for a single cappuccino. Cold milk gives you more time to work before you overshoot temperature.

**Submerge the tip.** Position the steam wand tip just below the surface, slightly off-centre. Not centred — that creates a symmetrical vortex and doesn't introduce air efficiently. Off-centre creates a rolling motion through the milk.

**Open the steam valve fully.** Don't ease in — full steam, immediately. Half-open valves create sputtering and uneven texture.

**Introduce air in the first ten seconds.** Lower the pitcher slightly so the wand tip breaks the surface by a few millimetres. You'll hear a paper-tearing sound — this is air being folded into the milk. This is the only time you want that sound. After ten seconds, raise the pitcher back so the tip is submerged again.

**Heat to 60°C.** Continue steaming with the tip submerged, creating a rolling motion in the milk. Stop when the pitcher is too hot to hold comfortably but not painful — roughly 60°C. If you use a thermometer, 60°C. If you don't, you will calibrate your hand to this temperature within a week.

**Tap and swirl.** Close the steam valve, set the pitcher down on the counter, and tap it firmly once. This pops any large bubbles at the surface. Then swirl the pitcher for ten seconds. This integrates the foam with the milk.

## What you're looking for

The milk should have a uniform sheen when you swirl the pitcher — no large bubbles, no grainy texture, no separation between liquid and foam. It should flow as a single body.

Pour it from 10–15cm height into a white cup. It should fall in a continuous stream with a slight gloss. If the liquid pours first and foam falls in a clump afterward, you've either over-foamed or under-integrated.

## The most common mistakes

**Steaming too hot.** Past 65°C the milk tastes scalded, slightly eggy. Once you've tasted it you'll recognise it everywhere. It's the most common flaw in café cappuccinos.

**Not purging.** The watery first second ruins texture. Always purge.

**Stopping too early on the swirl.** Thirty seconds of swirling is not too much. The foam and milk need to be integrated completely before you pour.

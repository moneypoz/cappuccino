The short answer: medium roast, pulled as espresso, holds up through milk better than either extreme. The long answer involves what you actually want from a cappuccino.

## What the milk does to the coffee

Steamed milk dilutes and rounds. It softens bitterness, adds sweetness, and insulates the espresso flavour behind a textural buffer. This is not a flaw — it's the point of the drink. The cappuccino is not espresso with milk added; it's a drink where milk and espresso are components of a specific balance.

The question for roast level is: what do you want the espresso to contribute after the milk has done its work?

## Dark roasts in cappuccino

Dark roasts — anything with significant oil on the surface, roasted to second crack — produce espresso with heavy body, low acidity, and prominent bitterness. With milk, the bitterness softens and the body persists. The result is the cappuccino most people who grew up on café coffee recognise: rich, slightly bitter, comforting.

The problem with dark roasts is that once you've tasted what's possible with better processing, they taste flat. The roast character dominates everything. You're tasting fire, not coffee.

## Light roasts in cappuccino

Light roasts preserve the origin character of the bean — fruity acidity, floral notes, complexity. In a filter brew, this is often stunning. In espresso for cappuccino, it's complicated.

Light roasts require more careful dialling — higher dose, longer ratio, finer grind — and they produce espresso that is higher in acidity and lighter in body. Through milk, the acidity softens but the body doesn't increase. A light-roast cappuccino can taste thin, or the acidic notes can clash with the milk's sweetness.

There are exceptions. A well-sourced washed Ethiopian at a true light-medium roast — developed enough to have body but preserving the stone fruit — can make an exceptional cappuccino. These coffees exist, but they require a roaster who knows what they're doing and a grinder dialled precisely. It's not the entry point.

## The medium roast case

Medium roasts have enough development to produce good body and crema, enough origin character to taste like something specific, and enough sugar browning from the Maillard reaction to hold up through milk with actual flavour.

For cappuccino specifically, a medium-roast blend or single origin with notes in the chocolate-to-caramel-to-nut range will produce a consistent result across most home machines and milk temperatures. This is where to start.

## What to look for on a bag

The language on specialty coffee bags is not standardised, so "medium roast" on one bag isn't the same as another. Better guidance:

- Look for a roast date within three weeks
- Look for beans with a matte surface, not shiny — oil on the surface means the roast went long
- For cappuccino, nudge toward espresso blends over single origins at first, then experiment
- Origin notes of chocolate, toffee, hazelnut, dried fruit: good starting points
- Origin notes of jasmine, bergamot, hibiscus: often beautiful filter coffee, needs careful handling as cappuccino espresso
